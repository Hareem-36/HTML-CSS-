Seerat-un-Nabi(The Life of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ)


1. Birth and Early Life

Born in Makkah in 570 CE, in the Hashim clan of the Quraysh tribe. Orphaned early; his father, Abdullah, died before his birth, and his mother, Amina, passed away when he was six years old.

2. Revelation

At the age of 40, received the first revelation from Allah through Angel Jibril (Gabriel) in the Cave of Hira on Mount Noor. This marked the beginning of his prophethood.

3. Message of Islam

Called people to believe in one God (Allah), abandon idol worship, and live righteous lives. Faced strong opposition from Quraysh leaders in Makkah.

4. Hijrah (Migration to Madinah)

In 622 CE, migrated to Madinah due to increasing persecution in Makkah. This event, called Hijrah, marks the beginning of the Islamic calendar.

5. Establishment of Islamic State

In Madinah, Prophet Muhammad ﷺ established the first Islamic state, uniting the tribes and laying down laws for governance, justice, and social welfare.

6. Battles and Struggles

Fought several significant battles including Badr, Uhud, and Khandaq (Trench), primarily defensive in nature. Despite hardships, the Prophet maintained patience and perseverance.

7. Treaty of Hudaybiyyah

A pivotal peace agreement made between Muslims and the Quraysh of Makkah in 628 CE, which eventually led to the peaceful conquest of Makkah.

8. Conquest of Makkah

In 630 CE, Makkah was peacefully conquered, and the Prophet ﷺ forgave his enemies, showing the ultimate mercy and compassion.

9. Farewell Sermon

During Hajj in 632 CE, the Prophet delivered his famous Farewell Sermon at Mount Arafat, emphasizing equality, justice, and the rights of all humans.

10. Death

Passed away in 632 CE in Madinah at the age of 63, after completing his mission of conveying the message of Islam.

Khatam-e-Nabuwat (Finality of Prophethood)


The concept of Khatam-e-Nabuwat (Finality of Prophethood) is central to Islamic belief, affirming that Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) is the last prophet. Here are key points about this doctrine:


  • Qur'anic Reference: The verse in Surah Al-Ahzab (33:40) explicitly states that Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) is the "Seal of the Prophets," marking the end of prophethood.
  • Prophet Muhammad's (PBUH) Role: As the final messenger, his teachings, the Quran, and Sunnah serve as complete guidance for all of humanity.
  • No New Prophets: After the Prophet Muhammad (PBUH), no new prophet will come. This is a fundamental belief for Muslims.
  • Unanimous Belief: The belief in the finality of prophethood is unanimously held by all major Islamic sects and scholars throughout history.
  • Historical Defense: Muslims have historically defended this belief, especially against any false claimants of prophethood after the Prophet Muhammad (PBUH).
  • Spiritual Completeness: Islam, as delivered by Prophet Muhammad (PBUH), is considered a complete and perfected religion that needs no further messengers.
  • Ahadith on Finality: Numerous hadiths confirm Prophet Muhammad's (PBUH) role as the last prophet, warning against false claimants.
  • Legal Framework: Several Islamic countries, including Pakistan, have legal provisions to protect the belief in Khatam-e-Nabuwat.
  • Spiritual and Moral Guidance: Muslims believe that the Quran and Sunnah provide comprehensive guidance for all aspects of life, without the need for new revelations.
  • Unity in Ummah: The belief in Khatam-e-Nabuwat is a point of unity for Muslims, reinforcing the shared adherence to the teachings of Prophet Muhammad (PBUH).

Hadith Books

Sahih al-Bukhari

Compiled by Imam al-Bukhari, this is considered one of the most authentic collections.

Sahih Muslim

Compiled by Imam Muslim. It is also highly regarded and ranks alongside Sahih al-Bukhari in authenticity and scholarly acceptance of hadith

Jami` at-Tirmidhi

Compiled by Imam Tirmidhi, it includes Hadiths categorized by topics like ethics, manners, and worship.

Sunan Abu Dawood

Compiled by Abu Dawood, it focuses on Hadiths related to practical aspects of life and jurisprudence.

Sunan an-Nasa'i

Compiled by Imam an-Nasa'i, this book includes Hadiths relevant to everyday life, and Islamic rulings.

Sunan Ibn Majah

Compiled by Ibn Majah, it is recognized as part of the “Kutub al-Sittah” or the six major collections.

Mohammed’s ﷺ Character reflected in his teachings

Amr bin Absar (May Allah be pleased with him)  was the stepbrother of  Abu Dhar Ghifari (May Allah be pleased with him). He was a shepherd. Even during his days of ignorance, he did not consume alcohol or worship idols. He used to say, “Because I used to abstain from these two sins, Allah blessed me. During the scorching heat of summer,  there used to be a piece of cloud over my head wherever I went in the desert. It was a shade provided by Allah.” He continued, “One day I came to know from people that something strange has happened in Makkah. A man from amongst them has declared himself to be the Messenger of Allah and is trying to stop them from worshipping idols and killing of daughters.

I went to Makkah and made enquiries about him. After meeting him, I embraced Islam and was probably the third or fourth Muslim at that time. It was the earliest period of Islam.

I asked him, “What is Islam?”  He replied, “Talking to people courteously and feeding the hungry.”

I asked him, “What is Emaan?” he replied, “As-sabru was-samaha; Being patient and being tolerant (towards others).”

 I asked again, “ Which is the best deed in Islam?” He replied, “To let people be safe from your tongue and hand.”

 I asked, “Which is the best part of Emaan?” He replied “To develop good conduct in one’s self.”

I asked again, “Which is the best prayer?” He replied, “The one which has a long qiyam (standing).”

 I asked, “ Which is the best Hijrat?” He replied, “One where a person abstains from deeds which displease Allah.”

 I asked again, “ Whose Jihad is the best?” He replied, “ One who goes to fight in the way of Allah and gets killed.”

I asked again, “Which is the best time to make dua?” He replied, “The last part of the night.”

The above hadith records that the earliest commands of Islam were mostly related to character building. 

    Talking politely and respectfully to others is a form of Islam.

     Sabr and Samaha – Being patient and tolerant towards others, and forgiving them for their mistakes is a part of Emaan.

    Abstaining from harming others, physically or verbally, is a part of  good conduct.

This is how Islam began training mankind to perfect the character, right from day one.
